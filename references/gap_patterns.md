# Research Gap Patterns

Use this to help students turn literature into problem awareness.

## Core Rule

A research gap is not "nobody has studied this." A good gap states:

- What prior studies already explain
- What remains unexplained
- Why that missing part matters
- How the proposed study can address it

## Gap Types

### 1. Black-Box Gap

Prior studies show X affects Y, but the mechanism is unclear.

Use when asking: "Why or how does this effect occur?"

Typical variable move: mediator.

Risk: Adding any middle variable without theory.

### 2. Boundary-Condition Gap

Prior studies show X affects Y, but not when, for whom, or under what conditions.

Use when asking: "When is this effect stronger, weaker, or reversed?"

Typical variable move: moderator.

Risk: Choosing a moderator only because it is easy to measure.

### 3. Context-Transfer Gap

A theory or finding is established in one context but not in another important context.

Use for country, industry, platform, culture, product category, or population shifts.

Risk: "Different country" alone is weak unless the context changes the theory.

### 4. Competing-Mechanism Gap

Two theories can explain the same outcome through different paths.

Use when comparing mechanisms rather than simply asking which variable is stronger.

Typical model: parallel mediators or competing paths.

Risk: Model becomes too complex for a master's proposal.

### 5. Contradictory-Findings Gap

Prior studies report inconsistent results.

Use when literature shows positive, negative, or null findings for the same relation.

Typical move: find boundary conditions, method differences, or construct differences.

Risk: Claiming contradiction without comparing designs and definitions.

### 6. Method Gap

Prior studies rely on methods that cannot answer a key question.

Examples:
- Cross-sectional surveys for causal claims
- Single case when comparison is needed
- Interviews when behavioral data is needed

Risk: Method novelty alone is weak unless it changes what can be learned.

### 7. Measurement Or Construct Gap

A concept is discussed, but definitions or measurements are unclear.

Use when constructs overlap, lack validated scales, or are used inconsistently.

Risk: Construct development is demanding; do not choose this for beginners unless scoped tightly.

### 8. Object Or Sample Gap

Research focuses on one group but ignores another theoretically important group.

Use when sample difference changes expected mechanisms.

Risk: "Students were not studied" is weak unless students matter theoretically.

### 9. Level Gap

Prior theory is at one level of analysis, but the proposed study needs another.

Examples: network-level theory used for individual psychology; firm-level strategy used for consumer intention.

Risk: Cross-level logic must be explicit.

### 10. Practice-Reinterpretation Gap

A real business phenomenon is important but not well explained by existing theory.

Use often in business/management proposals.

Risk: Practical importance is background, not academic contribution, unless tied to theory.

### 11. Assumption-Challenge Gap

Prior research shares an assumption that may be questionable.

Use for advanced students.

Risk: Requires strong literature command; avoid for L0/L1 unless simplified.

## Gap Quality Test

A gap is strong if the student can answer:

- Which prior studies create this gap?
- Is it a mechanism, boundary, context, method, measurement, or theory gap?
- Why does the gap matter academically?
- What would we know after filling it?
- Can the student's method actually address it?
