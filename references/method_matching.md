# Method Matching Rules

## Purpose

Ensure the proposed method can support the student's claim.

## Match Claim To Design

- Association claim -> survey, regression, SEM, correlation
- Mechanism claim -> mediation model; stronger with temporal or experimental design
- Boundary claim -> moderation/interaction model
- Causal claim -> experiment, quasi-experiment, longitudinal design, or clear identification strategy
- Process claim -> interviews, case study, process tracing, longitudinal qualitative data
- New construct claim -> conceptual definition, item generation, reliability, validity, distinction from related constructs

## Cross-Sectional Survey Warning

One-time survey data can support association and tentative mechanism. It should not be described as proving causality unless the design has strong justification.

Use cautious language:

- "is associated with"
- "is expected to influence"
- "the study examines the relationship"
- "the proposed model tests whether M explains the relationship"

Avoid:

- "proves"
- "causes"
- "confirms causal effect"

## Mediation Requirements

- Theory supports X -> M -> Y
- X should precede M, and M should precede Y
- The indirect effect is tested
- Alternative explanations are considered

## Moderation Requirements

- Specify which relation is moderated
- Explain the expected direction of the interaction
- Avoid unnecessary dichotomizing of continuous moderators
- Interpret interaction results, not just significance

## Moderated Mediation Requirements

- Specify which path is moderated: X -> M, M -> Y, or direct X -> Y
- Estimate conditional indirect effects
- Explain the condition theoretically
- Do not choose a PROCESS model number before forming theory

## Method Suggestions By Student Level

L0/L1:
- Focus on whether the question needs survey, interview, experiment, or secondary data.
- Avoid SEM/PROCESS unless teaching the concept first.

L2:
- Explain regression, ANOVA, mediation, moderation, and experiment fit.

L3:
- Discuss measurement validity, common method bias, endogeneity, robustness, and identification limits.

## Common Method Errors

- Listing methods without explaining why they answer the research question
- Claiming causality from cross-sectional survey
- Using SEM because it sounds advanced
- Using ANOVA without manipulated groups
- Calling any interview a case study
- Ignoring sampling and data access
